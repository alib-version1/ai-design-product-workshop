# Tips and Tricks for Working with AI Tools

These tools are more powerful than a search engine or a chatbot — but they behave differently. A few simple habits make a big difference. Every tip here is about helping you get the result you need, faster and with less frustration.

---

## Tip 1 — Start a new chat for every new task

The tool remembers everything said in a conversation. Old instructions can muddle new ones, and a chat that has been going for a while often loses focus.

When you move to a new task, open a new chat. It takes seconds and keeps the tool focused on what you actually need right now.

Example: "I've finished my start page. Now I want to build the next page — I'll start a fresh chat."

---

## Tip 2 — Give the tool context at the start

When you open a new chat, paste a short summary of your project before you ask for anything. The tool does not know your service, your users, or what you have built so far. Tell it.

Include: what you are building, who it is for, and what you want next.

Example: "I'm building a GOV.UK-style prototype for a housing benefit service. I have a start page. I want to add a question page that asks for the user's name."

---

## Tip 3 — Describe what you want to see, not how to build it

You do not need to know how something works technically. Just describe what the user should experience.

Good: "I want a button that takes the user to the confirmation page."

Less useful: "Add a link tag with an href pointing to confirmation.html."

Your job is to know what the service needs. The tool's job is to work out how to build it.

---

## Tip 4 — One thing at a time gets better results

Asking for five changes at once often produces something that misses all five. Make one request, check the result, then ask for the next thing.

If you have a list of changes, work through them one by one. It takes a little longer but gives you much more control over the outcome.

---

## Tip 5 — If the tool is going in circles, stop

Signs you are in a loop: the tool keeps suggesting the same fix, or keeps reintroducing the same problem after you ask it to fix something else.

More attempts rarely break a stuck loop. A fresh start usually does.

What to do: stop the chat, open a new one, and describe the problem from scratch. Try: "Start from scratch. The problem I am trying to solve is [describe it simply]."

---

## Tip 6 — Always review what the tool produced

The tool does not know if what it created is right for your users. You do.

Read the output before treating it as done. Does it say the right thing, in the right tone, for the right people? AI is confident — it does not flag its own mistakes. Your professional judgement is part of the process, not an optional extra.

---

## Tip 7 — When something looks wrong, describe what you expected

"It is broken" gives the tool very little to work with. Say what you expected to happen and what happened instead.

Example: "I expected the button to go to the next page. When I click it, nothing happens."

The more specific you are, the more useful the response.

---

## Tip 8 — Ask it to explain what it did

If you do not understand a change the tool made, ask: "Can you explain what you just added, in plain English?"

Understanding the changes helps you spot problems and makes your prompts better over time. It also helps you stay in control of what is in your prototype.

---

## Tip 9 — Use undo freely

In Kiro and GitHub Copilot you can reject a suggestion before accepting it. In your editor, Ctrl+Z (or Cmd+Z on Mac) undoes the last change.

Nothing is permanent. You can always undo, start again, or go back. Try things. The worst outcome is a result you do not keep.

---

## Tip 10 — You are in charge

The tool works for you. You do not have to accept every suggestion.

If something does not feel right for your service or your users, say so. Example: "That does not match how our service works. Can you try this instead: [your description]."

Your expertise in your users, your service, and your organisation is what the tool does not have. That expertise is what makes the output good.

---

## Try it now

Here is a quick scenario to practise two of the most important tips.

You have been working in a long chat. Your prototype has a start page, but now you want to add a new question page.

1. Open a new chat (Tip 1).
2. Before you ask for anything, paste this into the chat: "I'm building a prototype for [your service]. I have a start page at index.html. I want to add a question page that asks [your question]. The page should match GOV.UK style."
3. Review what it produces. Does the question make sense for your users? Does the tone fit?

If something is not right, describe what you expected (Tip 7) rather than just saying it is wrong.
